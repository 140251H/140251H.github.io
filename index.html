The Cold War

Evolving nature and character
The unlikely alliance between two mutually antagonistic ideologies: the U.S. and the Soviet Union was pivotal in defeating Nazi Germany. However, as there was tension between the USA and Soviet Union’s vastly different ideologies, the alliance that took place from 1941 to 1945 began to fall apart (Maynes, 1993). The Soviet Union followed the Communist ideology which featured a single state with a planned economy. In comparison to the U.S, which featured Capitalist ideology, based on a liberal democratic society, comprising a multi-party elected representative parliamentary system (USC, 2020). The contradictory ideologies contributed to a number of events, where the evolving nature and character of the Cold War was depicted. For instance, the ideologically opposed alliances NATO and the Warsaw Pact influenced the nature of the Cold War by increasing tensions (Richardson, 2018). Furthermore, the Red Scare or McCarthyism modelled the evolving character of the Cold War, where the speculation of Communist infiltration in the U.S caused a nation-wide hysteria. In addition, the Space Race showed the evolving character. The event was a high-stake and expensive competition between the superpowers, which was followed by the easing of tension during the period of détente (Siddiqi, 2000). The evolving nature and character of the Cold War was indicative of the dynamics between the two superpowers as the Cold War unfolded between 1948 to 1989.

NATO & the Warsaw Pact

In March 1948, secret meetings were held in the Pentagon, where American, British and Canadian representatives attended. It was recognised that the establishment of a security council was needed to provide security for the West, due to the possibility of Soviet obstructionism in the United Nations (NATO, 2012). The NATO agreement was signed in April 1949 and was led by the U.S. In 1954, the Soviet’s request for membership with NATO was rejected (Kaplan, 2004).

Figure 1: NATO flag. NATO member nations during the Cold War: five Brussels treaty nations, U.S., Canada, Denmark, Iceland, Italy, Norway and Portugal.

In response to their rejection for NATO membership. The Soviets were able to quickly respond to create their own military alliance which was named the Warsaw Pact. The Soviet-led alliance was signed in Poland on the 14th of May 1955 and allowed the Soviets to control other Communist states in Eastern Europe and prevent pact members from gaining autonomy (Storrs, 2005).

Figure 2: Warsaw Pact logo. The alliance consisted of 8 member nations: USSR, Poland, East Germany, Albania (to 1968), Bulgaria, Czechoslovakia, Hungary & Romania.

After 36 years of existence, the Warsaw Pact came to an end. NATO on the other hand, still exists today.

The Red Scare & McCarthyism

(McCarthy, 1953)

Influenced by the Red Scare, FBI director J. Edgar Hoover associated any form of protest with communist infiltration, including the civil rights demonstrations (Gentry, 2001). Throughout the 1960s, Hoover accused Martin Luther King Jr. of being influenced by communists, and so actively sought to discredit King, in what was a personally hostile campaign. Figure 1 explains the FBI’s campaign against King.

Figure 3: Video explaing the FBI's Counterintelligence Program that targeted many civil rights groups and activists in the 60s.

McCarthy was determined to undermine political opponents by questioning their loyalty to the United States. However, with no overt success in undermining political opponents. McCarthyism eventually came to an end in the mid-1950s as there was a loss of public popularity and opposition from the U.S. Supreme Court. McCarthyism demonstrated the mistrust and tension of American politics and military, in fear of Communist infiltration. American historian Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr. continues: “And one must hope that this brilliant reminder of a squalid past will help inoculate our occasionally distraught society against paranoid relapses in years to come” (Rovere, 1995). The Red Scare revealed the indirect character and the mistrusting nature of the Cold War as Communism was a threat to the U.S. but was never proven to be a threat in the U.S.

Space Race

(Dickson, 2001)

3rd November 1957: USSR launch Sputnik 2, carrying a dog named Laika (Laika pictured in figure 4) and became the first nation to successfully send a living organism into orbit.

Figure 4: Laika in a training capsule before her mission to space. The USSR became the first nation to successfully send a living organism into orbit.

1st October 1958: After President Dwight Eisenhower ordered the formation of a space agency in July 1958, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) was established in the U.S.

12th April: Soviet cosmonaut Yuri Gagarin made a single orbit around Earth and became the first man to reach space.

16th June 1963: Soviets sent the first woman, Valentina Tereshkova into space (refer to figure 5).

Figure 5: Soviets sent Valentina Tereshkova into space. She became the first civilian and woman in space.

President John F. Kennedy (JFK) ordered NASA to travel to and from the Moon. This decision further illustrated the competitiveness of the Space Race, as achieving this would put the U.S. ahead of the Soviets in the Space Race.

20th September 1963: If the proposed joint lunar expedition took place (refer to figure 6), an easing of tensions may have occurred.

Figure 6: In a speech to the United Nations General Assembly, JFK proposed a joint lunar expedition with the Soviets.

Unfortunately, on the 22nd of November, JFK was assassinated, so both sides continued with their separate plans.

18th March 1965: As seen in figure 7, Russian/Soviet cosmonaut Alexi Leonov became the first person to conduct a spacewalk (USC, 2020).

Figure 7: Footage of Russian/Soviet cosmonaut Alexi Leonov conducting the first spacewalk.

1967: Figure 8 demonstrates the consequences of the dangerous nature of the Space Race. It was perhaps one of the most competitive years between the superpowers.

20th July 1969: Figure 9 shows what was arguably the sign of victory for the Americans.

Commanders Tom Stafford and Alexei Leonov exchanged the first international handshake, which potentially symbolised the end of the rivalry between the Soviets and Americans, following by an era of Détente.

INFO MODAL (COLD WAR)
The Cold War was a period of geopolitical tension between the Soviet Union and the United States and their respective allies, the Eastern Bloc and the Western Bloc, after World War II. The period is generally considered to span the 1947 Truman Doctrine to the 1991 dissolution of the Soviet Union. The term "cold" is used because there was no large-scale fighting directly between the two superpowers, but they each supported major regional conflicts known as proxy wars. The conflict was based around the ideological and geopolitical struggle for global influence by the two powers, following their temporary alliance and victory against Nazi Germany in 1945.[1][2] The doctrine of mutually assured destruction (MAD) discouraged a pre-emptive attack by either side. Aside from the nuclear arsenal development and conventional military deployment, the struggle for dominance was expressed via indirect means such as psychological warfare, propaganda campaigns, espionage, far-reaching embargoes, rivalry at sports events and technological competitions such as the Space Race.
