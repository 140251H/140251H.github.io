The Cold War

Evolving nature and character
In the immediate the aftermath of World War Two (WWII), it was planned to have the world revolve around the United Nations economic reforms and cooperation. However, these decisions soon created difficulties, as there was tension between USA and Soviet Union ideologies. The Soviet Union followed the Communist ideology which featured a single state with a planned economy. In comparison to the U.S, which featured Capitalist, liberal democratic society, comprising a multi-party-political system and a representative parliamentary system. Due to the contradictory ideologies, the nature of the Cold War was composed of indirect conflict, mistrust and both direct and indirect rivalries. The evolving nature and character refer to how the dynamics between the two superpowers became more complex as the Cold War unfolded.

The Red Scare & McCarthyism

The second Red Scare took place from the late 1940s and throughout the 1950s in the United States. Also popularly known as “McCarthyism” after its main supporter Senator Joe McCarthy of Wisconsin (right-wing Republican), McCarthyism was an anti-Communist fervour that influenced American politics and culture.

Space Race

Space Race
The Cold War was a period of geopolitical tension between the Soviet Union and the United States and their respective allies, the Eastern Bloc and the Western Bloc, after World War II. The period is generally considered to span the 1947 Truman Doctrine to the 1991 dissolution of the Soviet Union. The term "cold" is used because there was no large-scale fighting directly between the two superpowers, but they each supported major regional conflicts known as proxy wars. The conflict was based around the ideological and geopolitical struggle for global influence by the two powers, following their temporary alliance and victory against Nazi Germany in 1945.[1][2] The doctrine of mutually assured destruction (MAD) discouraged a pre-emptive attack by either side. Aside from the nuclear arsenal development and conventional military deployment, the struggle for dominance was expressed via indirect means such as psychological warfare, propaganda campaigns, espionage, far-reaching embargoes, rivalry at sports events and technological competitions such as the Space Race.

INFO MODAL (COLD WAR)
The Cold War was a period of geopolitical tension between the Soviet Union and the United States and their respective allies, the Eastern Bloc and the Western Bloc, after World War II. The period is generally considered to span the 1947 Truman Doctrine to the 1991 dissolution of the Soviet Union. The term "cold" is used because there was no large-scale fighting directly between the two superpowers, but they each supported major regional conflicts known as proxy wars. The conflict was based around the ideological and geopolitical struggle for global influence by the two powers, following their temporary alliance and victory against Nazi Germany in 1945.[1][2] The doctrine of mutually assured destruction (MAD) discouraged a pre-emptive attack by either side. Aside from the nuclear arsenal development and conventional military deployment, the struggle for dominance was expressed via indirect means such as psychological warfare, propaganda campaigns, espionage, far-reaching embargoes, rivalry at sports events and technological competitions such as the Space Race.
